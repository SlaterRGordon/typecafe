# TypeCafe Vision

> **Monkeytype tells you how fast you are. TypeCafe makes you faster.**
> Every screen either proves that promise or gets out of the way.

This is the filter for every product decision. Before building anything, ask: *does this make someone measurably faster, or show them that they're getting faster?* If the answer is no, it doesn't ship — no matter how cool it is.

---

## The one-sentence pitch

TypeCafe is a typing coach, not a typing test. Tests are how we collect evidence; the product is the improvement loop built on top of them.

## Why this positioning wins

Every successful typing site owns one verb:

| Site | Verb | What they actually sell |
|---|---|---|
| Monkeytype | **Test** | A beautiful stopwatch. Deep customization, zero diagnosis. |
| Keybr | **Learn** | Adaptive lessons, but you're locked into its rigid flow and ugly UI. |
| TypeRacer | **Race** | Competition. Fun, but you don't improve — you just perform. |
| TypingClub | **Teach** | Curriculum for schools. Children's product. |

Nobody owns **improve**. Monkeytype measures but never diagnoses. Keybr diagnoses but never lets go of the steering wheel. The gap in the market is the full loop, run by the user, on their terms:

**Measure → Diagnose → Drill → Re-measure → See the delta.**

TypeCafe already has every raw ingredient: per-key accuracy tracked across sessions in every mode, smart drill, key-isolation practice, n-gram progression, a structured learn path, score history. What's missing is the connective tissue — and the conviction to put improvement, not testing, at the center of every screen.

A 7/10 Monkeytype clone loses to Monkeytype every time. A 9/10 typing coach has no competitor.

## Who it's for

1. **The plateaued intermediate (primary).** 60–90 WPM, takes tests constantly, hasn't gotten faster in a year. They don't need more tests — they need to know *why* they're stuck and what to do about it. Nobody serves them. They are the most numerous, most frustrated, and most likely to evangelize when a number that hasn't moved in a year starts moving.
2. **The keyboard enthusiast.** Benchmarks every new board, posts screenshots. Served by the share cards, heatmaps, and aesthetics. They're the acquisition channel.
3. **The beginner.** Served by Learn mode. They become plateaued intermediates — the pipeline feeds itself.

## Product principles

### 1. Every test is evidence, never just a score
A finished test that only reports WPM is a wasted test. Every completion should add to the user's model of themselves: which keys cost them speed, which bigrams broke their flow, whether their consistency is improving. The score is the headline; the diagnosis is the story.

### 2. Diagnosis must end in a button
"Your `r` is weak" is trivia. "Your `r` is weak → **[Drill it]**" is a product. Every insight anywhere in the app — score card, profile, progress page — links directly to the drill that fixes it. Never show a problem without selling the cure one click away.

### 3. Progression is the retention engine
Bests plateau in weeks; trends compound forever. The user's home for self-worth is not their top score, it's the chart going up: WPM trend, accuracy trend, the heatmap warming from red to green, the worst-bigram list shrinking. Strava doesn't retain runners with single-run stats. We don't retain typists with single-test scores.

### 4. The numbers must be beyond reproach
A typing coach whose measurements are wrong is a quack. WPM, accuracy, and consistency math gets unit tests, gets documented publicly ("how we calculate WPM"), and matches or beats the rigor of every competitor. The first Reddit comment on any launch will be someone comparing our WPM to Monkeytype's. We win that thread or lose the audience.

### 5. The differentiators live on the surface
No flagship feature hides behind a gear icon. Mode switching is visible on the main page. The keyboard heatmap is a destination, not an easter egg. If a first-time visitor can't tell TypeCafe from a minimal test clone in 10 seconds, the screen has failed.

### 6. Beautiful, minimal, fast — but never empty
We keep the aesthetic that earned the r/MechanicalKeyboards post. Minimal means no clutter; it doesn't mean no substance. A lone bigram floating in a void (today's grams mode) is not minimalism, it's neglect.

### 7. Sharing is bragging about progress, not just speed
"112 WPM" is a flex for the top 5%. "+18 WPM in 60 days" is a flex available to everyone — and it's a better ad, because it promises the viewer the same result. Share cards, profiles, and OG images all lead with deltas wherever a delta exists.

## What TypeCafe is not

- **Not a customization playground.** We will never out-theme Monkeytype. Themes serve the aesthetic; they are not the product.
- **Not a realtime arcade.** Live multiplayer racing is TypeRacer's moat and a populated-lobby problem we refuse to inherit. Competition at TypeCafe is asynchronous: daily challenges, beat-my-run links, leaderboards.
- **Not a school curriculum.** Learn mode is self-directed, for adults who want it, with none of the gamified-classroom cruft.
- **Not a test mill.** If a feature encourages mindlessly grinding 15-second tests with no feedback loop, it's working against the product.

## The promise, restated as UX

A user who gives TypeCafe 15 minutes a day should be able to say, four weeks later:

> "I know exactly which keys were holding me back, I drilled them, and I have the chart that proves I'm 12 WPM faster."

No other typing site lets anyone say that sentence. Every roadmap item ([roadmap.md](roadmap.md)) exists to make it true, faster, for more people.
